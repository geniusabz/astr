Melnikov’s masterpiece on Plyushchikha street: how architects are restoring Kauchuk club

The wonderful illustration of constructivist art to be returned to the city as it was originally built almost 100 years ago.

Each era gives Moscow a memorable architectural style; the Moscow Baroque was replaced by classicism followed by eclecticism of Alexander Kaminsky, which was then succeeded by Art Nouveau of Fyodor Schechtel and Lev Kekushev.

In the early Soviet era, the capital became the focus of constructivism that was characterized by regular geometric shapes free from decorations of the eclectic past. Almost every constructivist building in Moscow has become an architectural masterpiece and has been announced a cultural heritage site, these including cultural centers, office and residential buildings, industrial structures and entire villages, which are systematically revamped and integrated into the metropolitan environment as they were created almost a century ago.

Architects have already restored iconic erections, such as Bakery No. 5, the Izvestiya building, house-commune, building of the Ministry of Railways, Danilovsky department store and Central Union, while the House of Narkomfin project was awarded the Moscow Restoration award established by the Moscow Government, in 2020.

Factory club designed by Konstantin Melnikov

Another bright constructivism example in the capital is the club of the Kauchuk factory on Plyushchikha street, where restorers are taking comprehensive effort to preserve this cultural heritage asset.

The semicircular building was designed by architect Konstantin Melnikov and built in 1927-1929. Its front entrance is put forward as an individual single-story antechamber, with its flat roof topped by a rostrum that was used to make public speeches during festive demonstrations or rallies, while along the outer wall above the second floor there was a sundeck for visitors to have rest and relax. An essential part of a factory club at that time was certainly a gym; it is built on the side, and its 13-meter span is ceiled with steel frame structures without any intermediate support.

The club was overhauled for the first time in the early 1950s when the main premises were designed à la Stalinist Empire, the walls were decorated with plaster moldings and wall decoration, the lights were replaced, and the auditorium was re-equipped to screen films.

“That design can be seen in Watch Out for the Automobile directed by Eldar Ryazanov. The amateur theater rehearsal and premiere scenes were shot right in the club of the Kauchuk factory,” says Irina Sivtseva, Chief Architect of the restoration project.

In the 2000s, it accommodated a night club, which had a serious impact on its appearance. The facing was dilapidating, the brick masonry was deteriorating, while the ground floor and the basement were getting wet because of constant leaks in the pipes. Nevertheless, the original bulk of the building and some authentic structures, including a metal spiral staircase on the stage, have survived well.

What Kauchuk will look like after restoration

The restoration in the Kauchuk club started in 2022 to unveil a clerestory that was buried under the late ceiling of the auditorium, a few original window and door frames with authentic fittings in addition to wood flooring in the lobby and the auditorium.

“These and other findings will help restore and return the building to the city as it was originally built. We’ve also found fragments of the decorative stone plaster on the façades; we use them to recreate the texture and color of the original decor,” says Irina Sivtseva.

The core of the club’s planning concept will be the auditorium with a parterre and two upper circles with 417 seats. On all the floors, there will be free space in front of the entrances, and on the first floor, the restorers will recreate the original lobby arrangement together with glazed doors, while the former gym is to accommodate a rehearsal room.

Irina Sivtseva says that it is really challenging to adapt the building for a theater that meets today’s construction and fire safety standards.

“We need to place engineering systems and equipment on the limited area and to also think over some means of egress for spectators and personnel in case of an emergency. To do this, we had to isolate the intercommunication staircases, which were previously open, with fire-proof doors. All the engineering systems in the audience and in the hall will be hidden either inside wall ducts or dropped ceilings,” she said.

The experts will soon restore the auditorium’s skylight to illuminate the room with sunlight during the day and with artificial lighting at night. In addition, they will redone the metal spiral staircase in the auditorium to connect the parterre and two upper circles.

“We’ll restore the metal railings, concrete steps and ceramic tiles of the landings and will recreate the rendered finish of the façades, internal and outer doors, windows and ceiling luminaires for them to be as they once were,” says Irina Sivtseva.

After the reconstruction, Moscow will get another piece of avant-garde architecture created by Konstantin Melnikov.
